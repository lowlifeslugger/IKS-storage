KEY ASPECTS OF ANCIENT TOWN PLANNING/ IN INDUS VALLEY CIVILISATION 1. Strategic Site Selection: Town placement considered factors like water bodies, wind orientation, and topography to ensure sanitary conditions and climate advantage. 2. Grid-Pattern Layouts: Cities were meticulously laid out with streets intersecting at right angles, forming blocks. Streets were laid out in a straight line, intersecting at right angles (north-south and east-west), dividing the city into well-defined rectangular or square blocks. 3. Division of the City (Zoning): Cities were generally divided into two main parts: an elevated Citadel (or Acropolis) for public buildings, granaries, and the ruling class, and a Lower Town for residential and commercial purposes 4. Zoning and Allocation: Cities were often zoned, with specific areas allocated for markets, religious institutions, and residential quarters, sometimes based on social classes or professions. 5. Organized Residential Areas: Houses were arranged in an orderly manner, with distinct quarters for different social groups or professions. 6. Public Buildings and Facilities: Public baths, wells for communal water supply, markets, and temples were integral parts of the planned city 7. Water Management: Planning included well-structured water supply systems, with wells and sometimes public baths for managing water resources for residents 8. Advanced Drainage and Sanitation: Early civilizations developed systems to drain wastewater from houses into covered street drains, connected to soak pits to maintain hygiene and cleanliness. 9. Public Halls: Assembly halls (Sabha) were crucial components for community gatherings and events, as described in texts like the Vastu-shastra texts. 10. Granaries: Large granaries were essential for storing food, ensuring the city's food security. 11. Construction Materials: Uniformly sized, baked bricks were commonly used for building walls, streets, and drainage systems. 12. Fortifications: Cities were often fortified with protective moats or walls to enhance security against floods and other threats. 13. Moats: Some plans included moats around the city walls for added defense.

The Indus Valley Civilization saw the emergence of its first cities during the Bronze Age. South Indian Architecture Ancient Indian architecture is defined by several unique features: Symbolism and ornamentation: Designs are imbued with deep spiritual significance, representing cosmic order and religious themes. Structures are adorned with elaborate carvings, sculptures, and painted motifs depicting gods, mythological stories, and daily life. Mathematical precision: Ancient texts like the Vastu Shastra provided detailed architectural guidelines for harmonious proportions and spatial layouts, often based on complex mathematical and geometric principles. Climate responsiveness: Traditional buildings incorporated features to adapt to India's climate, such as thick walls for insulation, courtyards for natural ventilation, and jharokhas (overhanging balconies) for shade. Influence of religion: The architecture is deeply shaped by the religious beliefs of Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism. The distinct structural forms of stupas, viharas, and temples all served a religious purpose. Local materials: Construction primarily used locally available materials like mud, wood, brick, sandstone, and granite, leading to significant regional variations in style. Mauryan Period (c. 322–185 BCE) During this era, under Emperor Ashoka, stone became a prominent material, and religious structures flourished. Key features: Construction of rock-cut caves, monolithic stone pillars with edicts (Ashoka Pillars), and large stupas. Notable example: The Great Stupa at Sanchi, originally commissioned by Ashoka, and the rock-cut Barabar Caves. Rock-cut architecture (c. 3rd century BCE onwards) This unique style involved carving structures directly out of solid rock, showcasing remarkable craftsmanship. Key features: Cave complexes were used as residences (viharas) and prayer halls (chaityas) by Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain monks. Notable examples: The Ajanta and Ellora Caves feature intricate murals and sculptures, while the monolithic Kailasa Temple at Ellora was carved from a single rock. Temple architecture (c. 4th–12th centuries CE) During the Gupta and medieval periods, distinct temple architecture styles emerged, codified in texts like the Shilpa Shastras. Nagara style (North Indian): Key features: Characterized by a curvilinear tower (shikhara) over the sanctuary (garbhagriha). Notable example: Kandariya Mahadeva Temple in Khajuraho. Dravida style (South Indian): Key features: Defined by towering gateway structures (gopurams) and a pyramidal, multi-tiered tower (vimana). Notable example: Brihadeeswarar Temple in Thanjavur and the Shore Temple at Mahabalipuram. Vesara style (Mixed): Key features: A hybrid of Nagara and Dravida traditions, with features from both. Understanding mental health and psychology A holistic model: Ayurveda and Yogic philosophy use the trigunas as a psychological model for understanding an individual's constitution (manasa prakriti). Integrative therapy: Mental health professionals are exploring the integration of triguna theory with modern psychotherapy, such as Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR). This allows for a more culturally sensitive and holistic approach to treatment that addresses emotional, psychological, and spiritual dimensions. Mapping personality traits: Research has found correlations between the triguna model and Western personality theories like the Big Five personality traits. For instance, a predominance of sattva can be linked to high levels of conscientiousness and agreeableness, while rajas can correlate with traits like extraversion and openness. 2. Managing stress and promoting well-being Counteracting restlessness: Modern life is often dominated by a fast-paced, high-stress lifestyle driven by ambition and desire (rajas). The triguna model provides concrete practices, such as meditation and mindfulness, to calm the mind and reduce the anxiety that comes with excessive striving. Overcoming inertia: With constant digital stimulation, many people suffer from information overload and burnout, leading to a state of mental heaviness and lethargy (tamas). The framework encourages purposeful activity and early rising to overcome procrastination and dullness. Achieving inner balance: Instead of suppressing any single quality, the goal is to create a dynamic balance. The model explains that all three gunas are necessary for a functional life, with sattva for clarity, rajas for motivation, and tamas for rest. 3. A sattvic diet of fresh, whole foods promotes mental clarity, while rajasic (spicy, stimulating) and tamasic (processed, stale) foods can disrupt balance. This ancient wisdom offers guidance for modern wellness trends like mindful eating and a healthy diet. Ethical decision-making: The Bhagavad Gita connects the trigunas to ethics, explaining how our dominant qualities influence our actions and moral compass. In professional settings like medicine, the framework can be used to understand the motivations behind decisions, emphasizing the importance of sattvic qualities like wisdom and integrity for ethical practice. 4. A sattvic personality is associated with emotional intelligence and reliability, a rajasic person with ambition, and a tamasic person with a lack of motivation. Leadership and team dynamics: In a team, a balance of different gunas is needed for success. A sattvic leader fosters a harmonious environment, a rajasic individual drives action and change, and a tamasic person provides grounding and restraint.
